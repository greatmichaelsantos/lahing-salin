Indigenous Culture · Olongapo City
The Dororo of the Ayta Ambala
A Living Prayer, A Living People
Based on research by Aeron James R. De Leon · Lakandayang Journal, 2024
🏔️
About the Ayta Ambala
20,000 Years of Resilience

Deep in the mountains of Sitio Mampuweng, Olongapo City, Zambales, lives one of the Philippines' oldest and most resilient indigenous communities — the Ayta Ambala. Believed to have settled in the archipelago 20,000 to 30,000 years ago, they carry within them a culture so rich and so beautifully human that every prayer they whisper tells a story worth hearing.

🙏
Definition
What is a Dororo?

The Dororo is far more than a prayer. It is a living record of the Ayta Ambala's experiences, dreams, and unshakeable faith. Every word, every tone, every whispered syllable carries the weight of generations — their joys and struggles, their hopes and losses, their deep reverence for nature and the divine.

library_books The Three Types of Dororo
01
Communal Prayer
Dororong Ritwal
expand_more

A powerful communal prayer lasting up to an hour, led by a specially chosen elder who trains for two full years before performing it. It is chanted with melody, punctuated by sudden shouts, and witnessed in complete reverence — even smiling during the ritual is forbidden. Remarkably, this prayer exists in no written form — it lives only in the memory of those chosen to carry it.

⏱ Up to 1 hour 📜 No written form 👴 2-year training
02
Healing Prayer
Dororong Panganganito
expand_more

A healing prayer performed by the kagun — a spiritual healer chosen not by humans but by the spirits themselves. When illness cannot be explained by ordinary means, the kagun wraps the patient in red cloth and forcefully recites prayers to drive away the bad spirits causing harm.

🌿 Spirit healer (Kagun) 🔴 Red cloth ritual
03
Personal Prayer
Pansariling Dororo
expand_more

The most personal of the three — a quiet, intimate prayer whispered by an individual at any time, in any place. It asks for a good harvest, protection on mountain trails, gratitude for even the smallest blessings, and remembrance of loved ones who have passed.

🌾 Good harvest 🛡 Protection 🙌 Gratitude 🕯 Remembrance
hub Four Themes in Their Prayers
🌾
Abundant Harvests
They pray before planting, believing that a blessed land will yield generously.
🛡️
Protection
They pray before climbing mountains to trade — asking to be kept safe from snakes and unseen dangers.
🙏
Deep Gratitude
They express thankfulness even for the smallest portion of food, seeing every meal as a divine gift.
🕯️
Remembrance
They lovingly remember their dead — lighting candles, leaving food, asking departed loved ones to pray for them.
✨
Spirituality
A People of Remarkable Faith

The Ayta Ambala believe in Apo Namalyari — the supreme god who controls all of nature — whom many now also recognize as Apong Hesus, a beautiful blending of their ancient spirituality with Christianity. Rather than abandoning one for the other, they have woven both together into a faith uniquely and powerfully their own.

They believe spirits dwell in mountains, rivers, and forests. They follow strict food rules — never mixing forest meat with river food — as a form of deep ecological respect. They never take more from nature than they need. And before every journey into the mountains, they pray.

⚠️
Preservation
A Culture Worth Saving

Despite their incredible resilience, the Ayta Ambala face very real threats. Modernization, urbanization, and the rapid spread of technology put their traditions at serious risk. The Dororong Ritwal, with no written record, could disappear forever if its last keeper is gone.

Through Aeron James R. De Leon's research — using the Maka-Pilipinong Pananaliksik, a Filipino-centered approach that respects indigenous ways of knowing — these prayers, and the people behind them, will not be forgotten.

"Hay! Apu' Kung Kayêk" — Ay! Lord, have mercy on me.
📚 Source Citation
